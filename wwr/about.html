History

Whitewater rafting can be traced back to 1811 when the first recorded attempt to navigate the Snake River in Wyoming was planned. The river was given the nickname "Mad River" as it was found to be too difficult and dangerous to navigate without training, experience, or proper equipment.

Lt. John Fremont and Horace H. Day built the first rubber raft in the 1840s. They planned to survey the Rocky Mountains and Great Plains. It wasn't until the turn of the century that the first commercial rafting trip took place. On June 9, 1940, a successful trip through the Snake River Canyon was led by Clyde Smith. In later years, a modern resort hotel in Grand Teton National Park was constructed by John D. Rockefeller. Surplus war rafts that were 8 ft wide by 27 ft long were used. Only less than 500 people went that year but demands began to increase. Whitewater rafting companies started forming in the 1960s and 70s, with whitewater rafting being included in the 1972 Olympic games in Munich for the first time.
